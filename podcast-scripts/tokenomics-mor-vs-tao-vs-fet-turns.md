```
More is the Morpheus token -- fair launch, no pre-mine, no VC allocation. You earn it by contributing capital, compute, code or community effort. Supply cap of 42 million.
```

## TURN 05 — BOBSKI
```
So right away, the insider allocation story is completely different across all three.
```

## TURN 06 — RACHEL
```
And that's the single most important factor when evaluating these models. When insiders hold significant supply, their incentives diverge from other participants. They're incentivised to pump narratives to create exit liquidity, not to build sustainable infrastructure.

More has zero insider allocation. No insider selling pressure, full stop.

TAO has no formal insider allocation, but 5.38 million TAO were mined between January 2021 and October 2023 with very limited participants. Polychain Capital incubated the project. DCG and dao5 accumulated large positions early. The top 1 percent of wallets control about 90 percent of stake. The effect is similar to insider allocation even without the label.

FET had about 50 percent to insiders. And Ocean Protocol -- which was part of the alliance -- allegedly converted and dumped about 263 million FET on the open market. Over 10 percent of circulating supply.
```

## TURN 07 — BOBSKI
```
That Ocean situation is wild. But let's talk about how you actually earn with each token. What does participation look like?
```

## TURN 08 — RACHEL
```
With More, the main entry is as a capital provider. You deposit staked ETH or other assets, the yield flows to the protocol, and you receive More emissions in return. Your principal stays yours -- only the yield is diverted. There's also a Power Factor where you can lock your earned More for up to six years and get up to a 10.7x multiplier on emissions.

With TAO, since dee-TAO launched in February 2025, you stake TAO into individual subnets. Your TAO converts to that subnet's alpha token through an AMM. You earn based on the subnet's performance. Higher potential returns but you're taking on subnet-specific risk.

With FET, it's standard Cosmos staking with a 21-day unbonding period. You can also pay for agent services and compute on the various alliance platforms.
```

## TURN 09 — BOBSKI
```
So what actually drives value for each token? If the network grows, where does that value show up?
```

## TURN 10 — RACHEL
```
For More, it's compute access demand plus a structural burn. You hold More to access inference on the network -- the more you hold, the more compute you can use. And 50 percent of More from protocol-owned liquidity generation is permanently burned. So growing usage means more holding demand, and the burn steadily reduces supply.

For TAO, value comes from subnet quality and staking flows. As subnets attract more stake, they earn more emissions. Registration burns create friction. The halving in December 2025 cut emissions in half, strengthening the scarcity narrative. But -- and this is critical -- Bit-tensor generates zero external revenue. The entire model runs on emissions.

For FET, value comes from staking demand, agent adoption and AI services across the alliance platforms. But the platforms don't publish usage or revenue data. For a combined ecosystem that's been live since 2017, that opacity is a red flag.
```

## TURN 11 — BOBSKI

## TURN 12 — RACHEL

```
It works on the scarcity narrative and institutional interest. The 21 million cap, the halving, the Grayscale ETF filing -- these drive speculative demand. But the academic research is sobering. A 2025 paper found that stake weight is the primary driver of rewards -- correlation of 0.50 to 0.80 -- while AI output quality has only 0.10 to 0.30 correlation. The protocol rewards capital concentration more than quality contributions.
```

## TURN 14 — RACHEL
```
More scores highest on practical decentralisation. Four independent contributor groups, no single entity controls emissions or governance long-term. The main caveat is bootstrap-phase controls while the network is young.

TAO is moderate. Dee-TAO improved emission allocation by replacing the centralised Root Network with market-driven staking. But governance is still a Triumvirate of three Opentensor Foundation employees, and stake concentration is extreme -- Gini coefficient of 0.98.

FET is low. The Superintelligence Alliance is a Singapore-registered company with a formal board. Validator set capped at 70. Ocean's departure proved the governance is corporate, not community-driven. The tech stacks are still separate platforms despite the merged token.
```

```
More if you believe in the compute network thesis and want genuine fair-launch exposure. Highest alignment between tokenomics and decentralisation goals. Highest risk because the network is youngest and liquidity is thin.

FET if you want the most liquid, most institutionally accessible DeAI position and are less concerned about actual decentralisation. Be aware the alliance has fractured and the technology integration is incomplete.
```
