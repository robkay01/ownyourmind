```
Welcome back to the Own Your Mind podcast. I'm Bobski, and today we're tackling the revenue question in decentralised compute. Render, Akash and io.net -- three networks, three different revenue models, and a fundamental tension between freedom and returns. Rachel, why is revenue the question that matters?
```

## TURN 02 — RACHEL
```
And I'm Rachel. Because most DeAI tokens have no revenue. Their value comes entirely from emissions -- participants earn tokens funded by inflation, trade them for dollars, and the system works until it doesn't. The few projects that generate real revenue from real customers are worth studying closely, because revenue separates infrastructure from speculation.
```

## TURN 03 — BOBSKI
```
So let's get the headline numbers out. What are we looking at?
```

## TURN 04 — RACHEL
```
Render has by far the largest market cap of the three. Akash is an order of magnitude smaller. io.net is the smallest.

On revenue -- Render generates modest burn-derived revenue. Akash generates millions in fully verifiable on-chain revenue. io.net claims much higher annualised revenue, but that's self-reported.

The price-to-revenue ratios tell the story. The market is paying a massive premium for Render's brand and liquidity, and a massive discount for io.net's unverifiable numbers. Akash sits in the middle with verifiable economics.
```

## TURN 05 — BOBSKI
```
Let's start with Render. It scores highest on returns in our system but lowest on freedom. Why?
```

## TURN 06 — RACHEL
```
Because Render is, functionally, OTOY's product with a token attached. OTOY built OctaneRender, one of the most widely used GPU rendering engines in the creative industry. The Render Network is OctaneRender's distributed backend -- studios submit rendering jobs to GPU operators instead of buying their own render farms.

The burn mechanism is straightforward. When a creator pays for a rendering job, 95 percent of the Render tokens spent are permanently burned. 5 percent goes to OTOY as a service fee. Node operators receive newly minted Render from an emission pool.
```

## TURN 07 — BOBSKI
```
So burns have grown significantly year over year. That sounds great. What's the catch?
```

## TURN 08 — RACHEL
```
Monthly emissions still outpace monthly burns by a factor of roughly eight. The system is net inflationary today. It needs approximately 8x more rendering demand before it flips deflationary. That's a lot of rendering.

But the moat is real. OctaneRender has genuine Hollywood adoption -- Beeple, Apple Vision Pro, Stability AI. A hard supply cap with declining emissions gives a clear path to deflation at scale. And the exchange listings -- Binance, Coinbase, Kraken, OKX -- provide institutional-grade liquidity.
```

## TURN 09 — BOBSKI
```
And the freedom score is low because...
```

## TURN 10 — RACHEL
```
The network is permissioned -- operators must be approved by the Foundation. The rendering engine, node client, job routing and allocation algorithm are all proprietary closed-source software. OTOY's treasury holds 23.3 percent of supply with no vesting. If OTOY disappeared, the network would likely cease to function.

And here's the uncomfortable truth -- Render's returns score is high precisely because it's centralised. The proprietary rendering engine is the moat. The permissioned network ensures quality. Decentralisation would likely reduce output quality and weaken the revenue model.
```

## TURN 11 — BOBSKI
```
So centralisation is literally the product strategy. Now let's talk about Akash. Opposite approach?
```

## TURN 12 — RACHEL
```
Completely opposite. Anyone with qualifying hardware can become a provider -- no application, no approval, no permission. The entire stack is open source under Apache 2.0. On-chain governance has processed over 300 proposals with 42 percent voter turnout. If Overclock Labs disappeared tomorrow, the network would continue operating. That's the decentralisation test, and Akash passes it.

Revenue is growing significantly year over year, and every dollar is a Cosmos transaction you can trace on a block explorer. Venice.ai processes billions of inference tokens through Akash. ElizaOS uses AkashChat as its default inference API. Pricing is 70 to 85 percent cheaper than AWS SageMaker.
```

## TURN 13 — BOBSKI
```
So where does Akash fall short on returns?
```

## TURN 14 — RACHEL
```
No supply cap -- 8 percent annual inflation dilutes holders. Staking yields about 7.3 percent nominal, which nets negative 0.7 percent real yield after inflation. You're staking to avoid dilution, not to earn.

The approved burn-mint equilibrium mechanism hasn't activated yet. When it does, tenants will burn AKT to mint compute credits, creating deflationary pressure. But until it activates, there's no link between usage and token value. Liquidity is thinner too -- no Binance listing, and daily volume is a fraction of Render's.
```

## TURN 15 — BOBSKI
```
And then there's io.net. What's the story there?
```

## TURN 16 — RACHEL
```
io.net is the most aggressive of the three. Claims substantial annualised revenue, hundreds of thousands of registered GPUs, partnerships with Dell Technologies. Named enterprise customers with specific cost savings figures.

The problem is verification. The revenue figures are self-reported. After a 2024 Sybil attack that inflated GPU counts, verified daily active GPUs are a tiny fraction of the registered total. The market has noticed -- a very small market cap relative to claimed revenue is the market saying "we're not confident in these numbers."

The token is deep below its all-time high with significant unlocks ahead. No on-chain governance. The enterprise case studies are compelling if true, but "if true" is doing a lot of work.
```

## TURN 17 — BOBSKI
```
So what's the lesson from this comparison?
```

## TURN 18 — RACHEL
```
Revenue claims without full verifiability carry a discount. Akash's fully on-chain revenue is more valuable as an investment signal than io.net's self-reported revenue, because you can trace every dollar of one on a block explorer and you can't do the same for the other.

And freedom and returns are genuinely in tension for compute networks today. Render proves centralisation can produce better short-term returns. Akash bets that freedom produces more durable infrastructure. Both can be right on different timescales.
```

## TURN 19 — BOBSKI
```
But there's a long-term argument for Akash, right?
```

## TURN 20 — RACHEL
```
Three structural advantages. First, verifiable revenue builds trust. As institutional capital enters DeAI, auditable on-chain revenue will be a prerequisite. Render's opaque revenue reporting will become a liability.

Second, permissionless scales differently. Render needs Foundation approval for every new operator. Akash lets anyone join. At scale, that adds supply faster.

Third, the burn-mint equilibrium activation is a catalyst. When Akash has both verifiable revenue and a deflationary mechanic, the much lower price-to-revenue ratio compared to Render could make AKT the more attractive risk-adjusted position.
```
